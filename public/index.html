📋 Tax Year 2025

Understanding Your 2025 Taxes

Everything you need to file your 2025 return confidently by April 15, 2026 — documents, deductions, brackets, and more.

💡

What is a tax return? A form you send to the IRS each year reporting how much you earned in 2025. You calculate if you owe more taxes — or deserve money back (a refund). Deadline: April 15, 2026.

📄

Documents You Need

  • ✓W-2 from your employer (wages earned in 2025)
  • ✓1099 forms (freelance, interest, dividends, crypto)
  • ✓SSN or ITIN for each person on return
  • ✓1095-A if you had Marketplace health insurance
  • ✓Bank account info for direct deposit refund
  • ✓Receipts for deductible business/medical expenses
🖥️

How to File for 2025

  • ✓IRS Free File — Free if income under $84,000
  • ✓IRS Direct File — Now available in 25 states
  • ✓Tax software — TurboTax, H&R Block, TaxAct
  • ✓VITA — Free IRS help if income under $67,000
  • ✓CPA / Enrolled Agent — Complex situations
  • ✓Sprintax — For nonresident international filers
👤

Choosing Your Filing Status

  • ✓Single — Unmarried or legally separated
  • ✓Married Filing Jointly — Usually the best option
  • ✓Married Filing Separately — Sometimes better
  • ✓Head of Household — Single + dependent child
  • ✓Qualifying Surviving Spouse — 2 yrs after spouse's death
⚠️

Missed the deadline? File immediately even if you can't pay in full. The penalty for not filing (5%/month) is 10x worse than the penalty for not paying (0.5%/month). Request an extension by April 15, 2026 using Form 4868 — this gives you until October 15, 2026 to file, but NOT to pay.

💡

Deduction vs. Credit: A deduction reduces your taxable income. A credit reduces your actual tax bill dollar-for-dollar. Credits are almost always better. A $1,000 credit saves exactly $1,000. A $1,000 deduction saves $220 if you're in the 22% bracket.

🏠

  • ✓Married Filing Jointly: $31,500
  • ✓Head of Household: $23,625
  • ✓MFS: $15,750
  • ✓Over 65 or blind: add $2,000 per qualifying condition

🆕

One Big Beautiful Bill (OBBB) — Signed into law July 4, 2025. This legislation permanently extended TCJA provisions and added several new tax benefits. Here's what changed for Tax Year 2025:

📈

Higher Standard Deductions

The standard deduction increased significantly: $15,750 single (up from $14,600), $31,500 married (up from $29,200). This means more income is shielded from tax for most Americans.

👶

Child Tax Credit → $2,200

The Child Tax Credit is now permanently $2,200 per qualifying child under 17, up from $2,000. The refundable portion is $1,700. Phase-out begins at $400,000 MFJ / $200,000 single.

🏠

SALT Cap → $40,000

The state and local tax (SALT) deduction cap is raised from $10,000 to $40,000 for tax years 2025–2029. This benefits especially taxpayers in high-tax states like NY, CA, IL, NJ. Returns to $10,000 in 2030.

💼

No Tax on Tips

Qualifying tip income is now tax-free (up to $25,000) for workers in service industries — food service, hospitality, hair salons, etc. Must report on Schedule A. FICA taxes still apply.

⏰

No Tax on Overtime

Overtime pay is excluded from federal income tax for eligible employees. This applies to overtime wages paid for hours worked beyond 40/week under FLSA. The exclusion is for income tax only; payroll taxes still apply.

👴

Senior Deduction $6,000

New $6,000 above-the-line deduction for taxpayers age 65 and older. Phases out at $75,000 (single) / $150,000 (married). Stacks with the existing over-65 standard deduction bonus.

🚗

Car Loan Interest Back

Interest on auto loans is now deductible up to $10,000 for new vehicles assembled in the United States. Phases out for higher-income taxpayers. Applies to loans taken in 2025 and after.

📊

§199A Made Permanent

The 20% qualified business income (QBI) deduction for pass-through entities (S-corps, partnerships, sole proprietors) is now permanent. Previously set to expire in 2025. Key for business owners.

⚠️

Self-employed? Two hats, double tax. You pay both employee AND employer portions of Social Security + Medicare (15.3% SE tax). The good news: you deduct half of it from income, and §199A lets you deduct 20% of qualified business income (now permanent under OBBB).

📅

2025–2026 Estimated Taxes

  • ✓Q4 2025: January 15, 2026
  • ✓Q1 2026: April 15, 2026
  • ✓Q2 2026: June 16, 2026
  • ✓Q3 2026: September 15, 2026
⚠️

Owe $1,000+? You must pay quarterly or face underpayment penalties.

💼

  • ✓Home office (regular, exclusive use only)
  • ✓Vehicle: 70¢ per mile in 2025
  • ✓Phone & internet (business use %)
  • ✓Software, subscriptions, tools
  • ✓Professional development & courses
  • ✓Health insurance premiums (100%)
  • ✓Retirement contributions (SEP-IRA, Solo 401k)

  • ✓Schedule SE — Self-employment tax
  • ✓Form 1040-ES — Quarterly estimates
  • ✓1099-NEC — From each client paying $600+
  • ✓Form 8995 — §199A QBI deduction

🏢 Business Taxes · Tax Year 2025

Business & Entity Taxation

S-Corps, partnerships, C-Corps, ASC 740 provisions, PTE elections, estimated taxes, and year-end planning — all updated for Tax Year 2025.

💡

S-Corp basics: An S-Corp is a pass-through entity — income, losses, deductions, and credits flow through to shareholders' personal returns via Schedule K-1. The S-Corp itself pays no federal income tax, but must file Form 1120-S. Under the OBBB, the §199A 20% QBI deduction is now permanent for S-Corp shareholders.

💵

Reasonable Compensation

S-Corp shareholder-employees must pay themselves a reasonable salary before taking distributions. The IRS scrutinizes S-Corps that pay low wages to avoid FICA taxes.

  • ✓Salary is subject to FICA (7.65% employee + 7.65% employer)
  • ✓Distributions above salary are NOT subject to FICA
  • ✓IRS uses industry comparables to assess reasonableness
  • ✓Key case: Watson v. Commissioner (8th Cir. 2012)
  • ✓Document salary rationale each year in corporate minutes
📊

Shareholder Basis

Shareholders can only deduct S-Corp losses up to their stock basis + debt basis. Basis must be tracked annually — the IRS does not track it for you.

  • ✓Stock basis increases: capital contributions + income allocations
  • ✓Stock basis decreases: distributions + loss allocations
  • ✓Debt basis: direct loans from shareholder to S-Corp only
  • ✓Losses suspended if basis reaches zero — carried forward
  • ✓Track on Form 7203 (required since 2021)
📤

Distributions

S-Corp distributions are generally tax-free to the extent of basis. Excess distributions are taxed as capital gain. Order of basis reduction matters.

  • ✓Reduce stock basis first, then debt basis
  • ✓Distribution in excess of basis = capital gain
  • ✓AAA (Accumulated Adjustments Account) tracks E&P history
  • ✓Pro-rata distribution requirement for all shareholders
  • ✓State treatment varies — check each state
⭐

QSBS — §1202 Exclusion

Qualified Small Business Stock can exclude up to 100% of capital gains (up to $10M or 10x basis) from federal tax for eligible shareholders.

  • ✓Must be a C-Corp at time of issuance (not S-Corp)
  • ✓Gross assets must be under $50M at time of issuance
  • ✓Must hold stock for more than 5 years
  • ✓Original issuance only — not secondary purchases
  • ✓Certain industries excluded (professional services, finance)
📝

§199A QBI Deduction (2025)

S-Corp shareholders can deduct 20% of qualified business income — now permanent under OBBB signed July 4, 2025.

  • ✓Phase-in for SSTBs starts at $197,300 single / $394,600 MFJ
  • ✓W-2 wage limitation applies above phase-out thresholds
  • ✓W-2 limit: 50% of W-2 wages, or 25% wages + 2.5% UBIA
  • ✓Reasonable comp reduces QBI — key planning consideration
  • ✓Reported on Form 8995 or 8995-A
⚠️

S-Corp Eligibility Rules

S-Corp elections can be inadvertently terminated. Know the rules to stay compliant.

  • ✓Max 100 shareholders (family counts as one)
  • ✓Only US citizen/resident individuals, estates, certain trusts
  • ✓No nonresident alien shareholders
  • ✓Only one class of stock permitted
  • ✓Certain financial institutions and insurance companies excluded
📅

Key S-Corp deadlines: Form 1120-S is due March 15, 2026 for Tax Year 2025 (or September 15 on extension). S-Corp elections for 2026 must be filed by March 15, 2026. Late elections may be available under Rev. Proc. 2013-30.

💡

Partnership basics: Partnerships (and multi-member LLCs taxed as partnerships) file Form 1065 and issue Schedule K-1 to each partner. Partners pay tax on their allocable share of income — not distributions. This distinction is critical for cash flow planning.

📋

Schedule K-1 Breakdown

Each partner receives a K-1 showing their share of partnership items. Understanding K-1 boxes is essential for accurate individual return filing.

  • ✓Box 1: Ordinary business income/loss
  • ✓Box 2-3: Rental real estate income/loss
  • ✓Box 9-10: Net §1231 gain/loss
  • ✓Box 13: Other deductions (§179, charitable)
  • ✓Box 20: §199A QBI info for pass-through deduction
⚖️

Capital Accounts

Partnerships must maintain capital accounts for each partner. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) and 2020 regulations require tax basis capital account reporting on Schedule K-1.

  • ✓Tax basis capital = contributions − distributions + income allocations
  • ✓Negative capital account ≠ immediate taxable event (usually)
  • ✓Tax basis method required since 2020 tax year
  • ✓Section 704(b) book capital also maintained separately
  • ✓Ending capital account disclosed annually on K-1
💼

Guaranteed Payments

Guaranteed payments to partners are deductible by the partnership and taxable as ordinary income + self-employment tax to the recipient partner.

  • ✓Reported on K-1 Box 4 — separate from distributive share
  • ✓Subject to SE tax (15.3% on first $176,100, 2.9% above)
  • ✓NOT eligible for §199A QBI deduction
  • ✓Paid regardless of partnership income/loss
  • ✓Distinguish carefully from distributive share allocations
🔄

§754 Election & Basis Adjustments

A §754 election allows the partnership to step up (or down) inside basis upon a partner's death or transfer of interest — eliminating phantom income for buyers.

  • ✓§743(b): Inside basis adjustment on transfer of partnership interest
  • ✓§734(b): Inside basis adjustment on distribution
  • ✓Once elected, §754 applies to all qualifying events
  • ✓Election filed with Form 1065 for the year of transfer
  • ✓Critical for high-value partnerships with appreciated assets
📉

Loss Limitation Rules

Partner loss deductions are limited by four sequential rules — all must be satisfied before a loss can reduce taxable income.

  • ✓1. Basis limitation — Can't deduct more than outside basis
  • ✓2. At-risk rules §465 — Must be at economic risk
  • ✓3. Passive activity §469 — Material participation required
  • ✓4. Excess business loss §461(l) — $313,000 single / $626,000 MFJ limit in 2025
🏗️

Special Allocations

Unlike S-Corps, partnerships can specially allocate income and loss items disproportionately among partners — but must satisfy Substantial Economic Effect rules.

  • ✓Must have substantial economic effect under §704(b)
  • ✓Requires deficit restoration obligations or QIO
  • ✓Depreciation often specially allocated to tax-basis investors
  • ✓Preferred return structures common in real estate partnerships
  • ✓Document in partnership agreement — IRS will look here first
📅

Key partnership deadlines: Form 1065 is due March 15, 2026 for Tax Year 2025 (or September 15 on extension). K-1s must be issued to partners by March 15. Late K-1s cascade into late individual returns.

💡

C-Corp basics: C-Corporations are separate taxpaying entities — they pay corporate income tax at 21% flat rate, and shareholders pay tax again on dividends (double taxation). However, C-Corps offer unique advantages: QSBS exclusion, greater benefit flexibility, and easier access to institutional investment.

📊

2025 Corporate Tax Rates

  • ✓Federal corporate rate: 21% flat (TCJA, made permanent by OBBB)
  • ✓Corporate AMT: 15% CAMT on adjusted financial statement income for companies with 3-yr avg income over $1B
  • ✓Stock buyback excise tax: 1% on net stock repurchases
  • ✓State corporate rates vary: Iowa 8.4% → Nevada 0%
  • ✓State apportionment: most states use single-sales-factor formula
💰

Dividends Received Deduction (DRD)

Corporate shareholders can deduct a portion of dividends received from other domestic corporations — reducing effective tax on intercompany dividends.

  • ✓70% DRD — less than 20% ownership
  • ✓65% DRD — 20% or more ownership (not consolidated)
  • ✓100% DRD — 80%+ ownership (consolidated group)
  • ✓Subject to taxable income limitation
  • ✓Reported on Form 1120, Schedule C
📉

Net Operating Losses (NOLs)

  • ✓Post-2017 NOLs: carry forward indefinitely, limited to 80% of taxable income
  • ✓No carryback for most C-Corps (except farming, insurance)
  • ✓Pre-2018 NOLs: 20-year carryforward, no 80% limit
  • ✓§382 limitation applies after ownership changes (>50% over 3 years)
  • ✓Track NOL vintage carefully — different rules apply
📑

Key C-Corp Tax Forms

  • ✓Form 1120 — C-Corp annual income tax return (due April 15)
  • ✓Form 1120-W — Estimated tax worksheet
  • ✓Schedule M-1/M-3 — Book to tax reconciliation
  • ✓Schedule UTP — Uncertain tax positions (large corps)
  • ✓Form 5471 — Foreign subsidiary disclosure
⚠️

Double taxation planning: To minimize double taxation, C-Corp owners commonly use salary (deductible to corp), fringe benefits (deductible to corp), loans from corp, and S-Corp elections. The 21% rate + 23.8% qualified dividend rate = effective combined rate of ~39.8% — often higher than a pass-through structure for small businesses.

💡

ASC 740 (Income Taxes) governs how companies account for income taxes in financial statements under US GAAP. It requires recognizing both current and deferred tax effects — capturing tax consequences of events in the period they occur, regardless of when cash is paid.

📊

Current vs. Deferred Tax

  • ✓Current tax expense — Tax owed to IRS for the current year based on taxable income
  • ✓Deferred tax asset (DTA) — Future tax benefit (e.g. NOL carryforward, warranty reserve)
  • ✓Deferred tax liability (DTL) — Future tax obligation (e.g. accelerated depreciation)
  • ✓DTAs and DTLs arise from temporary differences between book and tax
  • ✓Permanent differences (meals, penalties) affect ETR only — no deferred tax
📉

⚖️

Valuation Allowance

A valuation allowance (VA) reduces a DTA when it is more likely than not (>50% threshold) that some or all of the DTA will not be realized.

  • ✓Evaluate positive and negative evidence each reporting period
  • ✓Positive: future reversing DTLs, strong earnings history, backlog
  • ✓Negative: cumulative losses in recent years, near expiration of carryforwards
  • ✓VA release can significantly boost reported earnings — scrutinized by auditors
  • ✓Documented in tax provision memo each quarter/year
📋

ETR Reconciliation

The effective tax rate (ETR) reconciliation bridges the statutory rate (21%) to the actual rate in the income statement — required disclosure under ASC 740-10-50.

  • ✓Start: pretax income × 21% federal statutory rate
  • ✓Add/subtract: state taxes, permanent differences, R&D credits
  • ✓Common items: §162(m) excess comp, meals (50% nondeductible), GILTI
  • ✓Discrete items: VA changes, statute expirations, return-to-provision
  • ✓ASC 740-10-50-15 requires % or dollar disclosure for items ≥5%
🔍

Uncertain Tax Positions (ASC 740-10)

Companies must evaluate whether each tax position meets a more-likely-than-not recognition threshold before recording any tax benefit.

  • ✓Step 1: Recognition — more likely than not to be sustained (>50%)
  • ✓Step 2: Measurement — largest amount with >50% cumulative probability
  • ✓Unrecognized benefits recorded as liability (not current tax)
  • ✓Interest & penalties: policy election — income tax or separate line
  • ✓Tabular rollforward of UTB balance required annually
📅

Provision Process & Timeline

  • ✓Q1-Q3 estimates: annualized ETR method or discrete-item approach
  • ✓Annual provision: full current + deferred calculation
  • ✓Return-to-provision: true-up when actual tax return is filed
  • ✓Audit support: maintain workpapers for all significant positions
  • ✓Coordinate with external auditors early — provision often on critical path
💡

Schedule C reports profit or loss from a sole proprietorship or single-member LLC disregarded entity. Net profit flows to Form 1040 and is also subject to self-employment tax (15.3%) on the first $176,100 and 2.9% above that for Tax Year 2025.

🏠

Home Office Deduction

A dedicated, regularly used business space qualifies. Two methods allowed:

  • ✓Simplified: $5 per sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500 max
  • ✓Regular method: actual expenses × business-use percentage
  • ✓Includes rent/mortgage interest, utilities, insurance, depreciation
  • ✓Employees working from home: NOT deductible since TCJA 2018
  • ✓Must be exclusive and regular use — no personal use allowed
🚗

Vehicle Deduction 2025

  • ✓Standard mileage rate: 70¢ per mile for 2025
  • ✓Must log date, destination, business purpose, miles
  • ✓Actual expense method: gas, insurance, repairs × business %
  • ✓§179 expensing: deduct up to $1,220,000 of vehicle cost in 2025
  • ✓Luxury auto limits apply to passenger vehicles
  • ✓Cannot switch from actual to standard after first year
💳

Deductible Business Expenses

  • ✓Advertising and marketing (100%)
  • ✓Professional fees — legal, accounting, consulting
  • ✓Office supplies, software, subscriptions
  • ✓Business meals: 50% deductible
  • ✓Business travel: 100% (flights, hotels, 50% meals)
  • ✓Professional development and education
  • ✓Health insurance premiums (100% above-the-line)
  • ✓Retirement contributions: SEP-IRA up to $70,000 in 2025
📊

§179 & Bonus Depreciation 2025

  • ✓§179 expensing limit: $1,220,000 for 2025
  • ✓§179 phase-out begins at $3,050,000 of purchases
  • ✓Bonus depreciation: 40% in 2025 (phasing down from 100%)
  • ✓Applies to new and used qualifying property
  • ✓Listed property rules limit deductions for vehicles, computers
📑

Schedule C vs. S-Corp: When to Convert?

The break-even analysis depends on net profit. A general rule:

  • ✓Under ~$40,000 net profit: Schedule C often simpler
  • ✓Over $40,000–$50,000: S-Corp election typically saves FICA taxes
  • ✓S-Corp costs: payroll, additional state filings, 1120-S prep
  • ✓Typical SE tax savings: 15.3% × (profit − reasonable salary)
  • ✓Consult a CPA — the math depends on your specific situation
🏦

Self-Employed Retirement Plans 2025

  • ✓SEP-IRA: up to 25% of net self-employment income, max $70,000
  • ✓Solo 401(k): $23,500 employee + 25% employer, max $70,000
  • ✓SIMPLE IRA: $16,500 employee deferral for 2025
  • ✓Solo 401(k) allows Roth contributions — major advantage
  • ✓SEP-IRA deadline: tax return due date (+ extension)
🆕

SALT cap update under OBBB: The federal SALT deduction cap was raised from $10,000 to $40,000 for tax years 2025–2029. However, the PTE (Pass-Through Entity) election remains valuable in many states because it can fully bypass the SALT cap for business income — not just raise it.

🗺️

What Is a PTE Election?

A Pass-Through Entity tax election allows a partnership or S-Corp to pay state income tax at the entity level instead of the individual level. The entity deducts the state tax on its federal return (no SALT cap applies to business deductions), and shareholders/partners get a state credit.

  • ✓Effective bypass of the federal SALT cap on business income
  • ✓Entity deducts state tax → reduces federal taxable income
  • ✓Individual gets a state credit for their share of PTE tax paid
  • ✓Available in 36+ states as of 2025
  • ✓IRS confirmed PTE elections are valid under Notice 2020-75

State Apportionment Basics

Multi-state businesses must apportion income among states. Most states now use a single sales factor formula.

  • ✓Single sales factor: Sales in state ÷ Total sales
  • ✓Three-factor: Average of sales, payroll, property (older states)
  • ✓Market-based sourcing: services sourced where customer receives benefit
  • ✓Cost-of-performance: services sourced where work is performed (older rule)
  • ✓Nexus created by: employees, property, economic nexus (sales threshold)
⚠️

SALT Planning with the $40,000 Cap

Even with the OBBB raising the SALT cap to $40,000, high earners in NY, CA, NJ, IL will still benefit from PTE elections for business income above the cap.

  • ✓$40,000 cap applies to individual SALT — not entity-level deductions
  • ✓PTE tax paid by entity: fully deductible, no cap
  • ✓Phase-out: SALT cap reduces above $500,000 MAGI
  • ✓NY PTET elections due March 15 for calendar-year entities
  • ✓Estimated PTE payments may be required quarterly
⚠️

Who must pay estimated taxes? Any individual or business expecting to owe $1,000 or more in tax after withholding must make quarterly estimated payments. C-Corps must pay if they expect to owe $500 or more. Underpayment penalty applies even if you pay in full by April 15.

Safe Harbor Rules — Avoid Penalties

Pay enough to qualify for a safe harbor and avoid underpayment penalties regardless of your actual tax liability:

  • ✓100% of prior year tax — safest if income was lower last year
  • ✓110% of prior year tax — required if prior year AGI over $150,000
  • ✓90% of current year tax — if you can estimate accurately
  • ✓C-Corps: 100% of prior year tax or 100% of current year tax
  • ✓Use Form 2210 to calculate underpayment penalty if needed

  • ✓Estimate annual gross income from all sources
  • ✓Subtract deductions: standard/itemized + business deductions
  • ✓Calculate tax using 2025 brackets
  • ✓Add self-employment tax (15.3% up to $176,100)
  • ✓Subtract credits and withholding
  • ✓Divide by 4 = quarterly payment. Use Form 1040-ES

Tips for Variable Income

Freelancers, partners, and S-Corp owners often have lumpy income. The annualized income installment method (Form 2210, Schedule AI) can reduce penalties when income is earned unevenly.

  • ✓Annualize actual income through each quarter-end
  • ✓Pay required amount based on annualized income — not equal quarters
  • ✓Best if most income earned in Q3/Q4
  • ✓Saves cash flow early in the year
  • ✓Pay via IRS Direct Pay or EFTPS at irs.gov

Common Financial Questions

Traditional IRA: Contribute pre-tax money, deduct it now, pay taxes in retirement. Best if you expect lower income in retirement.

Roth IRA: Contribute after-tax money. All growth and withdrawals in retirement are 100% tax-free. Best for younger workers or those expecting higher income later.

2025 Roth income phase-out: $150,000–$165,000 (single) / $236,000–$246,000 (married). Both: $7,000/year limit ($8,000 if 50+).

The 50/30/20 rule is a great starting point: 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings. For retirement specifically, aim to save 15% of income (including employer match). At minimum, always contribute enough to get the full employer 401(k) match — that's a guaranteed 50–100% return.

Simple decision tree:
1. Always get the full 401(k) employer match first (guaranteed return).
2. Pay off high-interest debt (>7%) before investing beyond the match.
3. Build 3-month emergency fund.
4. Max Roth IRA ($7,000/year).
5. Max 401(k) ($23,500/year).
6. Invest in taxable brokerage accounts after.

Credit score (300–850) = how risky you are to lenders. Components: Payment history (35%) — always pay on time. Utilization (30%) — keep balances below 30% of limits. Age (15%) — keep old accounts open. Mix (10%) — varied account types. Inquiries (10%) — limit applications. Free report: AnnualCreditReport.com

Getting a letter from the IRS is scary — but usually not as serious as it seems. Here's exactly what to do.

IRS Scam Warning — 2025: The IRS will NEVER call demanding immediate payment, threaten arrest, or ask for gift cards, crypto, or wire transfers. All real IRS contact starts with a letter in the mail. Scam calls are common — hang up immediately and report to reportphishing@irs.gov.

Common IRS Notices Explained

The most common IRS notice. It means the IRS found income reported to them (like a 1099 you forgot) that doesn't match your return. You have 60 days to: agree and pay, disagree with documentation, or partially agree. Don't ignore it — the IRS will assess the tax automatically if you don't respond.

🌍 International Students

Tax Guide for International Students

Filing US taxes on an F-1, J-1, or other visa is confusing. Here's exactly what you need to do for Tax Year 2025 (filed by April 15, 2026).

⚠️

Critical: Most international students are "nonresident aliens" for tax purposes — even after years in the US. You file different forms than citizens and cannot use TurboTax or H&R Block for nonresident filers. Use Sprintax or Glacier Tax Prep instead.

Step-by-Step: Tax Year 2025

1

Determine Your Tax Residency Status

Use the Substantial Presence Test. Most F-1/J-1 students are nonresident aliens for their first 5 calendar years. After that, you may become a resident alien and file a regular 1040.

2

Get an ITIN if You Have No SSN

No SSN and no US income? Apply for an ITIN via Form W-7. Your school's international office or an IRS Acceptance Agent can help. Processing takes 7–11 weeks.

3

File Form 8843 (EVERYONE Must)

ALL F-1, J-1, F-2, and J-2 visa holders must file Form 8843 for 2025, even with zero US income. Deadline: April 15, 2026. Mail to: Department of the Treasury, Internal Revenue Service Center, Austin, TX 73301-0215.

4

File Form 1040-NR (If You Had US Income)

Had wages, fellowship income, or US bank interest? File 1040-NR using Sprintax or Glacier (not TurboTax). Many universities provide Sprintax free. Deadline: April 15, 2026.

5

Check Your Tax Treaty Benefits

The US has treaties with 65+ countries. China, India, South Korea, Germany, France, UK, Canada and many more. A treaty may exempt scholarship, fellowship, or wage income. Claim using Form 8233 (wages) or on 1040-NR (other income).

By Visa Type

F-1
Student Visa

Nonresident first 5 years. OPT/CPT wages taxable. Can't claim standard deduction (except India treaty). No FICA on campus jobs.

J-1
Exchange Visitor

Nonresident first 2 of last 6 years. Includes scholars, researchers. Many treaty exemptions. File 8843 always.

H-1B
Work Visa

Usually resident alien after ~1 year. File regular Form 1040. FICA applies. Can use TurboTax. All standard deductions apply.

OPT
Optional Practical Training

F-1 OPT wages taxable (nonresident rules). FICA may be exempt during OPT. Keep strong documentation of status dates.

  • ✓Room & board, stipends: Taxable
  • ✓TA/RA wages: Taxable — withheld at 14%
  • ✓Treaty may exempt all of the above — check yours!

📖 Glossary

Finance Terms, Plain English

Every confusing term you've encountered — explained like a knowledgeable friend, not a textbook.

AGI
Adjusted Gross Income — your total income minus specific deductions like IRA contributions or student loan interest. Used to calculate many tax benefits.
Example: $60,000 income − $3,000 IRA contribution = $57,000 AGI.
W-2
Form your employer sends by January 31 showing wages earned and taxes withheld. You need this to file your 2025 return.
Box 1 = taxable wages. Box 2 = federal tax withheld.
1099
Form for income NOT from an employer — freelance work, bank interest, dividends, crypto. No taxes withheld, so you may owe more at filing.
Client paid you $2,500 for work → they send 1099-NEC.
FICA
Federal Insurance Contributions Act — 7.65% taken from every paycheck: 6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare. Your employer matches it.
On a $4,000 paycheck: $306 goes to FICA taxes.
OBBB
One Big Beautiful Bill — major tax legislation signed July 4, 2025. Raised standard deductions, boosted Child Tax Credit to $2,200, raised SALT cap to $40,000, eliminated tax on qualifying tips and overtime.
Affects all 2025 tax returns filed in 2026.
§199A / QBI
Qualified Business Income deduction — allows pass-through business owners (S-Corps, partnerships, sole proprietors) to deduct 20% of qualified business income. Made permanent by OBBB in 2025.
$100,000 in QBI → $20,000 deduction → saves ~$4,400 in taxes at 22%.
SALT Cap
State and Local Tax deduction cap — limits how much state income/property tax you can deduct on your federal return. Was $10,000. Raised to $40,000 for 2025–2029 by OBBB.
NY taxpayer paying $35,000 in state taxes can now deduct the full amount.
Roth IRA
Retirement account funded with after-tax money. Grows tax-free; withdrawals in retirement are 100% tax-free. 2025 limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+).
$7,000 at age 25 could grow to $100,000+ by retirement — all tax-free.
Capital Gains
Profit from selling an investment. Short-term (under 1 year): taxed as regular income. Long-term (over 1 year): taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20%. 2025: 0% rate applies up to $48,350 single.
Bought stock at $10, sold at $25 after 2 years → $15 long-term capital gain.
ITIN
Individual Taxpayer Identification Number — 9-digit number (starts with 9) for people who need to file taxes but can't get a Social Security Number. Apply via Form W-7.
International student with no SSN → apply for ITIN to file Form 8843.
PTE Election
Pass-Through Entity tax election — available in most states, lets an S-Corp or partnership pay state income tax at the entity level. Shareholders/partners get a credit, effectively bypassing the federal SALT cap for business income.
Available in 36+ states including NY, CA, IL, NJ, TX, FL.
Substantial Presence Test
IRS formula to determine if an international person is a "resident alien" for US taxes. Count: 100% of current year days + 1/3 of last year + 1/6 of two years ago. Need 183+ days total.
F-1 students are exempt from this test for their first 5 calendar years in the US.
